4 — digital drawing tool

A tool can be everything. It could make your life easier, it could make it harder. It could generate something, visualize something. I like visualising data, so I chose to make a tool for me and my hiking friends that visualises who has collected the most stamps in the Harz mountains. Who will become the first Harzer Wanderkaiser*in of the lot?

I had the idea to write a data visualisation tool that helps me and my four hiking friends to keep track of who is in the lead for our race to become Harzer Wanderkaiser*innen. I have had this idea for a while and was excited to spend some time on this project.

Katharina said, I should consider some other ideas as well. So I went onto brainstorming and came up with more ideas (see Fig.2):

  • a only connect riddle creation tool
  • a breath meditation tool
  • a "what to do instead of scrolling on my phone" generator
  • a hope generator, for when you are feeling hopeless
  • a mushroom game
  • a tool for timing my tea and tracking my tea collection
  • a tool that changes shapes according to the time
  • a tool that calculates how much money elon musk made during the time you spend on this website

This Wireframing exercise was really helpful to come up with many great ideas:

  1. My tool is a ... generator. A generator for visuals, posters, illustrations, type, words etc.
  2. It is a tool for ... me / us / kids / professionals / ...
  3. The tool makes ... easier / harder. everything / one specific thing / drawing / decisions / ideas / working / napping ...
  4. What shape(s) do you see on the website? Do you see typography or a photo, do you see simple shapes (circle, square, triangle) or complex ones (organic, fractal, composite), do you see a brush or a grid?
  5. What variables does the tool have? / What rules does the shape follow? Try sketching the logic (e.g., "if x then y") alongside the shape. Is the rule visible to the user or hidden behind the form?
  6. How can I change the shape? What is the role of the user? Does it respond to sliders, user input, time of day, random chance, sensor data, or network activity? Is it generative, interactive, participatory? Is the user a co-creator, a manipulator, or just a spectator?
  7. How does the shape look on mobile / desktop?
  8. What emotions, associations, or references does the tool create? What does it communicate? Does it feel playful, chaotic, meditative, structured?
  9. What is the role of randomness vs. control? Which parts are deterministic and which are unpredictable?
  10. What happens when the shape breaks the rules? Imagine a glitch, error, or exception in the algorithm.
    How does the wireframe represent failure, randomness, or overflow?

Maybe I will use some of those ideas in the future, for the moment I decided to go with the one most interesting and relevant to me: My hiking tool!


I decided to do some analogue visuals and craft the characters and background out of paper.

I decided to design some special cursors (Fig.5) for different uses.

  1. arrow: you can click
  2. hand: you can open something
  3. stamp: you can stamp
  4. crown: normal cursor
  5. tomato: not a cursor, but if you click it you find the source (sauce)
